PlastiCulture is a pop-up museum-type exhibit that invites visitors to explore the history of plastic. They will have the opportunity to look at how plastic use is harming the environment and our health, and in which industries it improves our quality of life. Participants will be challenged to increase their awareness of the use of plastics in all aspects of their lives, and to find ways to reduce their use of plastics. They will walk out of the exhibit with greater knowledge of the impact of different types of plastics on the environment, and with tools to make changes in their habits, as well as to encourage change in plastic use in local businesses and their communities.

Did you know that Canadians invented the plastic garbage bag?

In 1950, Harry Wasylyk of Winnipeg, Manitoba, and Larry Hansen of Lindsay, Ontario invented the green garbage bag in Wasylyk's kitchen. They heated and applied pressure to polyethylene pellets, which had been discovered in England in 1933 by Eric Fawcett and Reginald Gibson. Wasylyk and Hansen then blew the pellets into a long tube through a method called extrusion. Finally, the bags were sealed at one end. Their first customer was the Winnipeg General Hospital. The number of polio cases in Canada had been increasing since the end of World War II, and the hospital was looking for a way to contain contaminated materials. The plastic, green garbage bag solved the problem. With better sanitation, and the introduction of the polio vaccine in 1955, Canadian hospitals began to bring the epidemic under control.

Larry Hansen worked for the Union Carbide Company in Lindsay, which bought the invention from Wasylyk and Hansen and started manufacturing green garbage bags for use in Canadian homes in the late 1960s. This new convenience was called Glad Garbage Bags.

Plastic garbage bags revolutionized the garbage industry by allowing rotting and decaying matter to be contained, thus reducing disease and the rotten garbage smell in the streets. It did not take long, though, to realise that the plastic garbage bags were creating another problem. They did not break down quickly in the landfill. In fact, it is estimated that these first garbage bags will take 1000 years or longer to break down.

While on vacation in the Bahamas with his wife in 1969, Canadian professor and inventor from the University of Toronto, James Guillet, noticed cruise ship litter contained in plastic bags, floating in the ocean. Guillet had be researching how polymers reacted to sunlight, and realised that he could create a photodegradable garbage bag that would break down much more quickly in the landfill with the aid of sunlight. He patented his invention in 1971. Unfortunately, it was not well received initially.

Even Guillet's photodegradable garbage bags have not solved the issues with plastic garbage bags. Photodegradable and biodegradable plastic bags break apart and degrade, becoming microplastics in the landfills after time. These microplastics are then carried by wind and rain water into our waterways, and eventually the oceans. Sea creatures consume the microplastics, humans and land animals consume sea creatures, and through the food chain, we all end up with microplastics in our systems. Recent studies on the dangers of microplastics for human and environmental health indicate that they can carry toxic chemicals, heavy metals, and cause the release of endocrine disruptors, which can cause cancers and reproductive disorders. So, while the use of plastic garbage bags is convenient, and can help to contain and prevent some diseases, they also contribute to the cause of other health issues in human and animal populations. The answer to the problems is not simple.
